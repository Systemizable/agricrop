Potato

Time to grow: 90-120 days

Potatoes are a starchy root vegetable and a staple food in many parts of the world. Known for their adaptability, they can be grown in a range of climates and are nutrient-dense, providing essential vitamins like vitamin C, potassium, and B vitamins. Potatoes are grown from "seed potatoes," which are small or cut-up pieces of mature potatoes with at least one eye (bud).

×
  • Choose Your Seed Potatoes.
    Select certified disease-free seed potatoes. Avoid using supermarket potatoes, as they are often treated to prevent sprouting.
  • Prepare the Seed Potatoes.
    Cut larger seed potatoes into pieces, ensuring each piece has at least one "eye" (sprouting area). Let the pieces dry for 1-2 days to prevent rotting.
  • Prepare the Soil.
    Potatoes grow best in loose, well-drained soil that’s slightly acidic (pH 5.0-6.0). Add compost or organic matter to enrich the soil.
  • Plant the Seed Potatoes.
    Dig trenches about 4-6 inches deep and space them 12 inches apart.
    Place the seed potatoes with the eye facing up and cover them with soil.
  • Water Regularly.
    Keep the soil moist but not waterlogged. Potatoes need consistent watering, especially once they start flowering.
  • Hilling the Plants.
    When the plants are about 6-8 inches tall, cover them halfway with soil to protect the tubers from sunlight (which can cause greening and bitterness) and to encourage larger yields.
  • Harvesting.
    For new potatoes, harvest 2-3 weeks after the plants start flowering.
    For mature potatoes, wait until the vines die back, then gently dig up the tubers.
  • Storage.
    Cure potatoes in a dark, cool place for 1-2 weeks to toughen their skins. Store in a dark, cool, and dry place to maximize their shelf life.
